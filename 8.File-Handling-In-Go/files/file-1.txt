The vast cosmos contains billions of galaxies, each with millions of stars and planetary systems.
Astronomers use telescopes to observe distant celestial objects and study their properties.
The Big Bang theory explains how the universe began approximately 13.8 billion years ago.
Black holes are regions where gravity is so strong that nothing can escape, not even light.

Neutron stars are incredibly dense remnants of massive stars that have exploded as supernovae.

Gravity is the fundamental force that shapes the structure and evolution of the universe.
Electromagnetic radiation travels at the speed of light and includes radio waves, visible light, and X-rays.

Neptune is the windiest planet in our solar system with supersonic wind speeds.
Asteroids are rocky remnants from the early solar system located mainly in the asteroid belt.
Comets are icy bodies that develop glowing tails when approaching the Sun.
Meteors are meteoroids that burn up in Earth's atmosphere, creating shooting stars.
The habitable zone around a star is where liquid water could exist on a planet's surface.
Red giants are stars in late stages of their lives, expanding significantly.
White dwarfs are dense stellar remnants left after a star sheds its outer layers.
Stellar nucleosynthesis is the process by which elements are created inside stars.
The cosmic microwave background is radiation left over from the early universe.
Redshift indicates that distant galaxies are moving away from us due to universe expansion.
Gravitational lensing occurs when massive objects bend light from distant sources.
The speed of light is approximately 299,792 kilometers per second in a vacuum.
Spectroscopy allows scientists to determine the composition of distant stars and galaxies.
Aurora borealis and australis are caused by solar wind interacting with Earth's magnetic field.
The Van Allen radiation belts surround Earth and protect us from solar radiation.
Mars has two small moons named Phobos and Deimos.
Europa, a moon of Jupiter, may harbor a subsurface ocean beneath its icy crust.
Titan, Saturn's largest moon, has a thick atmosphere and liquid hydrocarbon lakes.
The Oort Cloud is a theoretical spherical shell of icy objects surrounding our solar system.
Kepler's laws describe how planets orbit around stars in elliptical paths.
Gravitational waves were first detected in 2015, confirming Einstein's predictions.
Accretion disks form around massive objects and are sources of intense radiation.
The cosmic scale is difficult to comprehend, with distances measured in light-years.
Parallax is a technique used to measure distances to nearby stars.
Absolute magnitude describes the intrinsic brightness of celestial objects.
The Andromeda Galaxy is our nearest large galactic neighbor at 2.5 million light-years away.
Radio astronomy reveals objects invisible to optical telescopes.
Infrared astronomy allows observation through dust clouds that block visible light.
X-ray astronomy studies high-energy phenomena like black holes and supernova remnants.
Gamma-ray bursts are among the most energetic events in the universe.
The Event Horizon Telescope captured the first image of a black hole's shadow.
Tidal forces caused by gravity differences can stretch and deform celestial bodies.

Stellar classification systems organize stars by their spectral characteristics and temperatures.

Binary stars orbit around their common center of mass.

Parallax measurements formed the foundation for the cosmic distance ladder.

The Hubble constant measures the rate of universe expansion.

Orbital mechanics govern how objects move in space under gravitational influence.

Interstellar travel requires propulsion technologies beyond our current capabilities.
Generation ships could transport humans across interstellar distances over centuries.
